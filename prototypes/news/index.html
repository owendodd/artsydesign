The News

January 28

The Los Angeles County Museum of Art announced plans for a satellite campus in the historically underserved South Los Angeles neighborhood.

The design for the as-yet-uncompleted sculpture, Bouquet of Tulips, was donated by Koons to the French capital in November 2016 as a memorial to the recent terrorist attacks that have taken place in the city. But Koons, one of the world’s richest living artists, didn’t donate the $4.3 million needed to create 40-foot-tall sculpture, which was raised separately via donations. And the work, slated for installation in front of the Palais de Tokyo and Paris’s Museum of Modern Art, has attracted opposition. In a letter published in the French newspaper Libération on Sunday, signatories—including Frédéric Mitterrand, the country’s former culture minister—demanded that the city halt its plans to install the sculpture, calling it “shocking.” It further criticized Koons for using the opportunity as a publicity stunt, as the sculpture’s planned location is in a tourist-heavy area far from where the 2015 terrorist attacks actually took place. “We appreciate gifts, [but ones that are] free, unconditional, and without ulterior motives,” the letter said. In any case, Parisian officials have not yet granted authorization to install the sculpture, according to the New York Times.

The oldest known depiction of a supernova was found in a work of 5,000 year old rock art, scientists believe.

Image courtesy of the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research

Scientists found the rock art, dates by to 3600BC, in Kashmir region of south Asia. Beyond being the first object to capture a supernova, the artwork “is likely the oldest sky chart ever drawn,” QZ reported. Astrophysicist Mayank Vahia determined that a suprnova did indeed occur in the region around the time the rock art was created, explaining the presence of two suns in the sky. The figures under the two suns in the work also correspond with constelations in the night sky, lending further evidence to Vahia’s theory. Further research will be conducted on other works of rock art in the area to see if a second depiction of the supernova can be found and confirm Vahia’s hypothesis.

For the first time in 950 years the famed Bayeux Tapestry will be loaned to Britain—and you can bet on where it will end up.

French President Emmanuel Macron agreed to loan the fabled Bayeux Tapestry to the United Kingdom in a decision announced at an Anglo-French summit in Britain on Thursday. The move symbolizes the continued strength of relations between the two countries even amid the forthcoming exit of the U.K from the European Union in 2019. A major historical object for both Britain and France, the 68-meter (223-foot) tapestry depicts the 11th century Battle of Hastings between Norman-French and English troops. It was created shortly after the battle and has has not left France in 950 years. The loan won’t begin until 2022 so that preservation work can be done to ensure the tapestry can be moved safely.

Interestingly, the Guardian reported, you can bet on where it will be shown:

It is understood the [British Museum] has been in talks about a possible loan of the tapestry for several years, but there will be other contenders to host it.

The bookmaker Ladbrokes is offering odds on where it might go, with the British Museum evens favourite, followed by Westminster Cathedral at 3/1, Canterbury Cathedral at 5/1 and Hastings at 8/1.
The Guardian

This is not the first time Ladbrokes, the popular U.K. betting service, has allowed art world watchers the potential to win big. The company allows you to bet on which of the annual turner prize nominees will walk away with the award. But those who put markers on Lubaina Himid,this year’s eventual winner, didn’t make much. The far and away favorite, Himid came with the rather unrewarding 4:5 odds.

Richard Prince shook up the legal team representing him against a copyright infringement lawsuit that could have major ramifications for artists.

The news that Richard Prince shook up his legal team, first reported by Law360’s Bill Donahue in a tweet monday, comes four months after the appropriation artist lost a motion to dismiss a major copyright infringement suit brought against him by artist Donald Graham in 2015.

Cariou v. Prince is one of this year’s most important cases to watch in the art world. In 2015, Graham sued Prince and his dealer, Larry Gagosian, after Graham’s 1996 photograph Rastafarian Smoking a Joint appeared in a work in Prince’s Instagram series exhibited at Gagosian gallery in 2014. Prince moved to dismiss the case, asserting fair use, but a judge shot down that motion in July 2017. Though a motion to dismiss comes with a high legal bar (the facts are interpreted in a way most favorable to Graham), the ruling “wasn’t good for Prince,” writes Donahue.
